Tracking Gun Violence

Apparently, there have been more mass shootings this year than there have been days. Maybe. There’s more to unpack here.

It depends on what you classify as a mass shooting event.

But here is the heart of the matter: how best do we frame these numbers to argue for or against gun control?

And here is another question: considering that mass shootings are actually very, very rare, why do they provoke so much more fear and anxiety in the American consciousness than shootings of the non-mass variety? Why do these incidents receive so much media attention when (thousands other) gun deaths are ignored?

Some hypotheses:

  • Mass shootings are more rare. People are more interested in rare things, like gemstones and collectibles.
  • Mass shootings take place in public (or semi-public) venues—schools, clinics, churches. They bring out the it could happen to me genre of disquiet.
  • Speaking of it could happen to me, there’s something about the arbitrariness of victims that marks them as especially innocent. Public mourning tends to require that victims be innocent: see, for instance, the response to the Colorado Springs Planned Parenthood shooting, where some commentators withheld their sympathy for women whom they considered murderers.
  • And this:

Gun violence disproportionately affects people of color. Put another way: mass shooting incidents disproportionately affect white people.
